Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

To implement collisions with spheres, we followed the algorithm line by line, calculating the unit normal, would-be collision point, and correction vector. For planes, we take the dot product of the plane's normal with the vector from a point on the plane to the mass's position, which is positive or negative depending on which side of the plane the point mass is on. We compare signs to see if the mass went through the plane, in which case we bounce it back to the other side of the plane.
Comparing the cloths with different spring constants, we see the cloth get stiffer as we increase the spring constant. At first, it looks like a thin scarf or some other fabric, but at 50,000 more like construction paper or some plastic.

Part 5: Shaders

Shaders take advantage of GPUs to parallelize parts of the graphics pipeline and speed up rendering. Vertex shaders modify/transform vertices of the geometry as well as their properties. These vertices are passed to fragment shaders, which process the vertices and output color for the fragments in the geometry. The Blinn-Phong shading model splits the lighting into three distinct components. They are ambient lighting, diffuse lighting, and specular. This makes it more realistic than a model of only diffuse lighting because the surface can now have glare.
